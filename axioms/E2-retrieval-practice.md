# E2 · Retrieval practice and desirable difficulty strengthen — at a delay

Testing does more than measure memory — it *strengthens* it. The **testing effect**
(also called the retrieval-practice effect) is one of cognitive psychology's most
replicated findings: a test trial produces better long-term retention than an
additional study trial, even when the two groups spend equal time with the material.

The critical qualifier: the advantage is **delay-dependent**. On an immediate test,
re-study and retrieval practice perform similarly or the study group may score
higher (because they just saw the material again). The testing group's advantage
emerges only at a delay — one day, one week, one month. This is the "desirable
difficulty" mechanism: the effort of self-testing encodes the trace more durably,
even though it feels harder and produces worse immediate performance.

**KPM implication.** A KPM lifecycle that only *reads* knowledge (study mode) will
observe high short-term accuracy but suffer accelerated retrievability decay.
Periodic re-validation challenges (spaced, low-stakes retrieval probes) should be
built into the package-lifecycle: retrieve without first re-reading the generator,
then compare output to the stored axiom. The testing-effect literature directly
informs [[D1-retrievability-decay]]'s decay-resetting mechanism: each successful
retrieval probe resets the retrievability clock more effectively than a re-read.
